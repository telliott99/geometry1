\label{sec:nine_point_circle}

The perpendicular bisectors of the sides of a triangle intersect (are concurrent) at a point which is the center of the circumcircle, the circle that goes through all three vertices.  

A second important point is where the angle bisectors intersect, giving the center of the circle that has the sides as tangents --- the incircle.  (This point can be found by construction; that it exists is easy to prove using the angle bisector theorem and the converse of Ceva's theorem).

\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.3] {ninepoint_rot_circ.png} \end{center}

Now we investigate the altitudes and their point of convergence, the orthocenter.  It turns out there is a special circle involving the feet of the altitudes, but it does not have the orthocenter as its center.  Quite surprisingly, there are nine special points on the circle. 

Starting with $\triangle ABC$, find the midpoints of the sides to form $\triangle KLM$.  By the midpoint theorem, we have four congruent smaller triangles.  These are similar to the original $\triangle ABC$ with ratio 1/2.

$\triangle KLM$ is called the medial triangle for $\triangle ABC$.

Find the circumcenter $N$ of $\triangle KLM$ in the usual way and draw its circumcircle through $K,L,M$.  The circumcircle for $\triangle KLM$ is is the nine point circle for $\triangle ABC$.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics [scale=0.25] {ninepoint_center.png}
\includegraphics [scale=0.25] {ninepoint_reflect.png}
\end{center}

Next, we want to rotate $\triangle KLM$ by 180 degrees around the point $N$.  One way to do that construction is to extend the diagonals through $N$.  Form the three possible rectangles, each will have one side from $\triangle KLM$ and an opposite side from $\triangle PQR$.

%\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.25] {ninepoint_reflect.png} \end{center}

We can show that $\triangle PQR$ is congruent to the other smaller triangles including $\triangle KLM$.  $\triangle PQR \cong \triangle KLM$ by SSS and $\triangle PQR \sim \triangle ABC$.  For example, $\angle AML = \angle Q = \angle B$.

Hence $ML \parallel QR$ and $ML \parallel BC$ by the midline theorem so $QR \parallel BC$.  This can be extended to the other sides of $\triangle ABC$ as well.

Notice that the stubby little triangles like the one with vertex $P$ and base formed by $ML$ are also similar to the others.

\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.25] {ninepoint_basic.png} \end{center}

We come now to the altitudes.  

Before going further, we claim that if in two congruent triangles we drop altitudes from corresponding vertices, the two sub-triangles so formed in each are congruent.  (\emph{Proof}.  We have equal acute angles in right triangles that also share one side.  $\square$).

\subsection*{the altitudes and the nine point circle}

The nine point circle from above also goes through the points where the altitudes of a triangle meet the sides, called the feet.  

The same circle also goes through the midpoints of that part of each altitude lying between the orthocenter and the corresponding vertex.

\emph{Proof}.

Draw the altitude $AD$.  Since $AD \perp BC$ also $AD \perp ML$ (at $S$) and $AD \perp QR$, it follows that $AS$ is the altitude from $A$ of $\triangle AML$.

We draw $PT$ as the altitude of $\triangle PQR$ and want to show that $PT$ lies on $ASHD$.  By the preliminary work, $\triangle ASM \cong \triangle PTQ$.

\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.25] {ninepoint_intersect.png} \end{center}

Since $MLRQ$ is a rectangle, $MSTQ$ is also a rectangle with $MS = QT$.

So the bases of $\triangle ASM \cong \triangle PTQ$ are parallel and equal, and the vertices at $M$ and $Q$ lie on the side of the rectangle $MLRQ$.  

It follows that $PS$ lies on $AHD$.

Since $MP \parallel KR$ and $MP \perp PR$ and $PR \parallel AC$, we have the extension of $MP$ is the altitude from $M$ in $\triangle AML$.  That is, $P$ is the orthocenter of $\triangle AML$.

Now since $PK$ is a diameter, and $\angle KDP$ is right, it follows that $D$ lies on the circle.  The same is true for $E$ and $F$.

Thus, each of the three altitudes of $\triangle ABC$ has its foot lying on the nine point circle.

The last part is to show that $AP$ is one-half the distance from $A$ to the orthocenter.  
\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.3] {ninepoint_rot.png} \end{center}

Since the parent $\triangle PQR$ is similar to $\triangle ABC$ with ratio 1/2, it follows that$\triangle HPQ \sim \triangle HAB$ with the same ratio.  Thus, $AP = PH$

$\square$

\subsection*{center of nine point circle}

I found from reading Coxeter that the center of the nine point circle bisects the Euler line.  Recall that the centroid $G$ lies one-third of the way along the Euler line from the circumcenter $O$ to the orthocenter $H$.  Then $N$ bisects $OH$ and $G$ lies one-sixth of that length away from $N$, on the side toward $O$.

\emph{Proof}.

We established above that $\triangle KLM \cong \triangle PQR$.  They share the same circumcircle on center $N$, since all the vertices lie on the circle, which is the nine point circle for the parent $\triangle ABC$.  

The two smaller triangles are related by rotation through a half-turn on center $N$.


\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.3] {ninepoint_rot_circ.png} \end{center}

Find the circumcenter of $\triangle ABC$ by dropping perpendiculars from points $K$, $L$ and $M$ to meet at $O$.  The same lines are the altitudes of $\triangle KLM$, since for example, $ML \parallel QR \parallel BC$.

The circumcenter of $\triangle ABC$, point $O$, is the same point as the orthocenter of $\triangle KLM$.  Rotation about $N$ converts $O$ for $\triangle ABC$ into $H$ for both $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle PQR$, and vice-versa.  It follows that the distances are the same:  $HN = ON$.

$G$ lies on the same line, close to $N$ on the same side as $O$.

$\square$
